Legal differences between separation and divorce in Connecticut

Often, couples in Connecticut who are considering divorce are not sure whether they should pursue legal separation or move forward with a full divorce. Understanding the legal differences between a legal separation and a divorce is crucial, especially when deciding between separation vs divorce and determining which option may be best for you.

Generally, a legal separation is best for couples who want to take time apart but do not actually want to get divorced. This is especially true for a couple who feels that their marriage is headed downhill, but they are not yet ready to file for divorce. In the context of separation vs divorce, the legal separation process also allows for a trial period to see if the marriage can be saved, which is not always possible in a divorce.

To qualify for a legal separation, at least one spouse must have lived in Connecticut for the past year or longer. The same laws that govern a divorce also apply to a legal separation, so the same rules and regulations regarding property distribution, alimony, and child custody will still be in effect. This highlights an essential aspect of separation vs divorce; both follow similar legal protocols but lead to different marital statuses.

However, it is important to note that a legal separation is not considered a divorce by the court, so you can technically continue living with your spouse while separated. In the debate of separation vs divorce, you will, however, be required to submit a legal separation agreement to the court that clearly states the terms of your breakup. Additionally, you cannot remarry during a legal separation. If you do decide to resume your marriage after a legal separation, you can ask the court to convert the legal separation into a divorce.

It is also important to remember that, even after a legal separation is granted, you will not be able to claim that your spouse committed fault-based grounds for divorce. When considering separation vs divorce, it's vital to note that fault grounds, such as intolerable cruelty, habitual drunkenness, or adultery, are not available during a legal separation. However, you can claim that your marriage has irretrievably broken down and there is no possibility of reconciliation with your spouse during a legal separation.
